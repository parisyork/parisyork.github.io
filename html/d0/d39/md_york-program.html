This adds greatly more information about the world around than humans can perceive, namely simultaneously viewing the real spatial world with boundaries and the space within those boundaries. Much as Abbott[FOOTNOTE] described seeing a three dimensional creatures view of Flatland which allowed the interior of the Flatlanders to be seen along with the boundaries. This view however, is that of a two dimensional surface embedded in three dimensional space; as the Dolphins view is a three dimensional surface embedded in four space. Seeing the interior simultaneously with the boundaries does not provide a complete understanding of four dimensional objects in four space, lacking are several key points including a complete view of four space rotations and objects that can only exist in four space.

The three projection views – Top, Front, Side (plan, front, side) [DIAGRAM]

In the 3D case, each view is a projection of a three dimensional object into a two dimensional viewing field. Standard 3D computer graphics do this, rending what is in the view frustum into a 2D mapping that can be displayed on the screen. One dimension is lost in the projection to create the 2D representation. There are two methods of projection depending on the lines of projection. Only the orthographic projections will be used in this application. Orthographic projections use lines of projection that are parallel. The parallel projection can be treated as a perspective projection if the far plane of the viewing frustum was at the infinity point.

When the projection is performed, one dimension is lost. Three dimensional objects have two projection views necessary to recreate the object because of the loss of one of these dimensions. In the projection display, it is necessary to have at least two orthogonal views of the object to recreate it.

Any combination of two of the three views, results in all three spatial dimensions being represented. The final view (typically the side view) is included to enhance understanding. 

Each projection of the 4D object is orthographic (projection lines parallel). The projections into three space are often referred to as shadows. A familiar shadow is that of the wireframe hypercube. Each projection has one less dimension than the whole object just as in the 3D case.

Can we rotate the object on arbitrary axes? No. We can rotate arbitrarily about a point, but not as easily as defining a line to be the axis of rotation as in the 3D case. DEFINITION Four–dimensional space is the set E4 of ordered quadruple (x, y, z, w), where x, y, z, w are real numbers.

The quadruple (x, y, z, w) is called a point P in E4 and we write P =(x,y,z,w).

The numbers x, y, z, w are called, respectively, the x, y, z, w coordinates of P.

The coordinate axes are the sets of points: {(x, 0, 0, 0)} (x–axis), {(0, y, 0, 0)} (y–axis), {(0, 0, z, 0)} (z–axis), {(0, 0, 0, w)} (w-axis).

The only point common to all four axes is the origin O = (0, 0, 0, 0).

The coordinate hyperplanes are the sets of points:

(Hyperplanes in E4 are E3 such that the space is divided in two; any hyperplane of a Euclidean space has exactly two unit normal vectors)

{(x, y, z, 0)} (xyz–plane), {(x, y, 0, w)} (xyw–plane), {(x, 0, z, w)} (xzw–plane), {(0, y, z, w)} (yzw-plane).

The positive octant consists of the points (x, y, z, w), where x > 0, y > 0, z > 0, w > 0.

We think of the points (x, y, z) with z > 0 as lying above the xy–plane, and those with z < 0 as lying beneath the xy–plane.

We think of the points (x,y,z,w) with w > 0 as lying “ana” xyz-hyperplane, and those with w<0 as lying “kata” the xyz-plane.

Charles Howard Hinton coined the terms ana and kata for the names of movement along the w-axis.
